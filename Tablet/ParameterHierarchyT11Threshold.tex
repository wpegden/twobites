\begin{helper}
For every \(\eta>0\) and \(0<\varepsilon_1\le1\), there is \(n_0\) such that
for every \(n>n_0\), if \(L=\log n\), then
\[
\frac{2000(5(1+\eta))^2(\log L)^2L^3}
{\varepsilon_1(1+\eta)\sqrt{nL}}\le1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Put \(\kappa=1+\eta\).  Since \(\kappa>0\), choose a positive constant
\[
c=\varepsilon_1(100000\kappa)^{-1}
\]
small enough that
\[
2000(5\kappa)^2c^5\le\varepsilon_1\kappa.
\]
This last inequality follows from \(0<\varepsilon_1\le1\) and
\(\kappa\ge1\).  Since \(\log n=o(n^{1/10})\), after increasing \(n_0\) we
may assume, for every \(n>n_0\), that \(n\ge1\), \(L=\log n\ge1\), and
\[
L\le c\,n^{1/10}.
\]
For such \(n\), \(\log L\le L\), hence
\[
(\log L)^2L^3\le L^5\le c^5\sqrt n.
\]
Multiplying by \(2000(5\kappa)^2\) gives
\[
2000(5\kappa)^2(\log L)^2L^3
\le \varepsilon_1\kappa\sqrt n.
\]
Because \(L\ge1\), we have \(\sqrt n\le\sqrt{nL}\).  Therefore
\[
2000(5(1+\eta))^2(\log L)^2L^3
\le \varepsilon_1(1+\eta)\sqrt{nL},
\]
and division by the positive denominator proves the claimed bound.
\end{proof}
