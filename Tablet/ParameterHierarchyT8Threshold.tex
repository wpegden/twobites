\begin{helper}
For every \(\eta>0\), there is \(n_0\) such that for every \(n>n_0\), if
\(L=\log n\), then
\[
\frac{200(5(1+\eta))^2(\log L)^2L^3}
{(1+\eta)\sqrt{nL}}\le1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Put \(\kappa=1+\eta\) and choose
\[
c=\frac1{10000\kappa}.
\]
Since \(\eta>0\), we have \(\kappa\ge1\), and this choice gives
\[
200(5\kappa)^2c^5\le\kappa. \tag{1}
\]
Because \(\log n=o(n^{1/10})\), after increasing \(n_0\) we may assume
\[
L=\log n\le c n^{1/10}
\]
for every \(n>n_0\).  We also require \(n\ge1\) and \(L\ge1\).  Then
\(\log L\le L\), so
\[
(\log L)^2L^3\le L^5\le c^5\sqrt n.
\]
Multiplying by \(200(5\kappa)^2\) and using (1) gives
\[
200(5\kappa)^2(\log L)^2L^3\le \kappa\sqrt n.
\]
Since \(L\ge1\), \(\sqrt n\le\sqrt{nL}\), and hence
\[
200(5\kappa)^2(\log L)^2L^3
\le \kappa\sqrt{nL}.
\]
The denominator \(\kappa\sqrt{nL}\) is positive, so dividing by it proves the
claimed threshold.
\end{proof}
