\begin{helper}
Let \(L=\log n\).  Assume \(\eta>0\), \(n>0\), \(L>0\), and
\[
(1+\eta)\sqrt{nL}\le k.
\]
If
\[
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\eta(1+\eta)}4\sqrt n\,\sqrt L\,L\right)\le\varepsilon_1,
\]
then
\[
\exp\!\left(-\frac\eta4 kL\right)\le\varepsilon_1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Since \(\eta/4\ge0\) and \(L\ge0\), multiplying the lower bound on \(k\) by
\((\eta/4)L\) gives
\[
\frac\eta4(1+\eta)\sqrt{nL}\,L\le \frac\eta4 kL.
\]
Because \(n>0\) and \(L>0\), we have
\[
\sqrt{nL}\,L=\sqrt n\,\sqrt L\,L.
\]
Thus
\[
\frac{\eta(1+\eta)}4\sqrt n\,\sqrt L\,L\le \frac\eta4 kL.
\]
Negating reverses the inequality, and the exponential function is increasing,
so
\[
\exp\!\left(-\frac\eta4 kL\right)
\le
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\eta(1+\eta)}4\sqrt n\,\sqrt L\,L\right)
\le\varepsilon_1.
\]
\end{proof}
