\begin{helper}
For every \(\eta>0\) and \(\varepsilon_1>0\), there is \(n_0\) such that
for every \(n>n_0\), if \(L=\log n\), then
\[
\frac1{(1+\eta)\log L}\le\varepsilon_1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Since \(\log\log n\to\infty\), choose \(n_0\) so that for every \(n>n_0\),
\[
\log L\ge \frac1{(1+\eta)\varepsilon_1}.
\]
Multiplying by the positive constant \(1+\eta\) gives
\[
\frac1{\varepsilon_1}\le (1+\eta)\log L.
\]
Both sides are positive, so taking reciprocals reverses the inequality:
\[
\frac1{(1+\eta)\log L}\le
\left(\frac1{\varepsilon_1}\right)^{-1}=\varepsilon_1.
\]
\end{proof}
