\begin{helper}
For every \(\eta>0\) and \(0<\varepsilon_1\le1\), there is \(n_0\) such that
for every \(n>n_0\), if \(L=\log n\), then
\[
\frac{400000(5(1+\eta))^2(\log L)^2L^5}
{\varepsilon_1(1+\eta)^2n}\le1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Put \(\kappa=1+\eta\).  Since \(\kappa\ge1\) and
\(0<\varepsilon_1\le1\), choose
\[
c=\varepsilon_1(100000\kappa)^{-1}
\]
so small that
\[
400000(5\kappa)^2c^7\le\varepsilon_1\kappa^2.
\]
Since \(\log n=o(n^{1/10})\), after increasing \(n_0\) we may assume that
for every \(n>n_0\), \(n\ge1\), \(L=\log n\ge1\), and
\[
L\le c\,n^{1/10}.
\]
For such \(n\), \(\log L\le L\), and therefore
\[
(\log L)^2L^5\le L^7\le c^7n^{7/10}\le c^7n.
\]
Multiplying by \(400000(5\kappa)^2\) gives
\[
400000(5(1+\eta))^2(\log L)^2L^5
\le \varepsilon_1(1+\eta)^2n.
\]
The denominator is positive, so division proves the claimed inequality.
\end{proof}
