\begin{helper}
For every \(\varepsilon_1>0\), there is \(n_0\) such that for every
\(n>n_0\), if \(L=\log n\), then
\[
\frac1{\sqrt L}\le \frac{\varepsilon_1}{2}.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Since \(\log n\to\infty\), we may choose \(n_0\) so that
\[
\log n\ge \left(\frac2{\varepsilon_1}\right)^2
\]
for every \(n>n_0\).  Put \(L=\log n\).  The right hand side is
nonnegative, so \(L\ge0\).  Therefore the monotonicity characterization of
the square root gives
\[
\frac2{\varepsilon_1}\le \sqrt L.
\]
Both sides are positive.  Taking reciprocals reverses the inequality and
gives
\[
\frac1{\sqrt L}\le \left(\frac2{\varepsilon_1}\right)^{-1}
=\frac{\varepsilon_1}{2},
\]
as required.
\end{proof}
