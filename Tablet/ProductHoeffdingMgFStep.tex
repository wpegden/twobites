\begin{helper}
Let \(\Omega\) be finite and let \(q:\Omega\to\mathbb R\) be nonnegative with
\(\sum_x q(x)=1\).  Let \(X:\Omega\to\mathbb R\) satisfy
\(a\le X(x)\le b\) for every \(x\), and suppose
\(\sum_x q(x)X(x)=0\).  Then for every \(\lambda\ge0\),
\[
\sum_x q(x)e^{\lambda X(x)}
\le
\exp\!\left(\frac{\lambda^2(b-a)^2}{8}\right).
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
The weights \(q\) define a probability mass function on the finite set
\(\Omega\), because they are nonnegative and sum to \(1\).  With respect to
this probability measure, the expectation of \(X\) is \(0\) and \(X\) is
almost surely contained in the interval \([a,b]\).

Hoeffding's lemma for a mean-zero random variable supported in an interval of
length \(b-a\) gives that \(X\) has subgaussian moment-generating function
parameter \((b-a)^2/4\).  Equivalently, for every real \(\lambda\),
\[
\mathbb E e^{\lambda X}
\le
\exp\!\left(\frac{\lambda^2(b-a)^2}{8}\right).
\]
Writing the finite expectation out as the weighted sum over \(\Omega\) gives
exactly the displayed inequality.
\end{proof}
